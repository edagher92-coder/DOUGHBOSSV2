Executive Summary

SNOWBOSS is a halal-certified frozen-dessert daypart (slush + soft-serve) proposed to operate inside the existing, trading Dough Boss Bankstown pizza shop — trading as Dough Boss 8am–1pm and "flipping" to SNOWBOSS from 1pm, opposite the $2B Bankstown Hospital development and a combined ~11,000-student university/TAFE precinct. It was scoped in the 12 June 2026 "DOUGH x SNOW — SNOWBOSS Business Proposal v3.2" as a joint venture between Charlie (Dough Boss Bankstown — site, lease, staff, brand) and Elie (Snow Flow — machines/product at cost, servicing, marketing, innovation pipeline), targeting a grand launch the week commencing 20 July 2026.

This document extends that proposal. It does not re-litigate the original numbers; it (a) verifies what has changed in the three weeks since 12 June, (b) builds a genuine standalone-path comparison so the joint-venture case can be judged against a real alternative rather than assumed, and (c) adds five analyses the original proposal didn't fully develop: a turnover report, a collaboration-revenue analysis isolating what the partnership itself is worth, a profit analysis, a BAS/GST analysis, a SWOT, a team & management structure recommendation, and a Q&A stress-test from four adversarial perspectives (accountant, operator, investor, competitor).

Headline recommendation

Proceed with the joint venture — the capital, speed, and catchment case is decisive and quantified, not just "further along." Standalone requires an estimated $60k–$130k+ in pre-trading capital against a multi-month timeline with zero proof of demand; the JV requires $10,800 to finish a build that is already ~85% paid, against a six-week timeline, on a site with independently corroborated demand drivers. But this is conditional, not unconditional: the section-10 partnership agreement — share split, staff-time compensation, servicing valuation, licence fee, exit/key-person terms — must be signed before the 20 July grand launch, not attempted and left open. Full reasoning in Section 11.

Every dollar figure in this document is traced to either (i) the 12 June 2026 v3.2 proposal, quoted as stated, (ii) externally verified research current as of 1 July 2026, or (iii) an explicitly labelled analyst estimate/recalculation shown with its derivation. Nothing is presented as more precise or more certain than its source warrants — projections are marked PROJECTED, joint-venture-specific facts JV, standalone-only reasoning STANDALONE, and structure-independent facts CONCEPT throughout.

Standalone vs Joint Venture — Comparative Analysis

There is no existing standalone proposal, site, or lease — everything site-specific in the standalone column below is an explicitly flagged estimate, not a quote or a real deal. Only three things carry over from the source document as real, verified figures regardless of path: Snow Flow's supply-at-cost menu economics (~91% GP), the SilverChef Rent-Try-Buy equipment channel, and the FWC minimum-wage figures (Section 2). Everything involving Charlie's site, lease, staff, brand base, or the Bankstown catchment belongs to the joint-venture path only.

Standalone — Pros

  • 100% ownership — no shareholders' agreement, no split of brand IP or profit with a partner.
  • Zero JV governance risk — no $2k sign-off threshold, no exit-clause negotiation, no partnership-dispute exposure.
  • Free choice of site, format, and brand — not constrained to a shared day/night identity inside someone else's shop.
  • No dependency on Charlie's business decisions — immune to "what happens if Dough Boss Bankstown is sold."
  • Keeps everything Snow Flow already owns independently: at-cost supply, servicing capability, innovation pipeline, SEO/marketing system.
  • SilverChef channel is not exclusive to the JV — the same confirmed equipment-finance terms are available standalone.

Standalone — Cons

  • Full market-rate lease from scratch — no discount, plus a bond (commonly 1–3 months' rent, NSW retail leasing convention).
  • Full fit-out from zero — electrical, counter, POS, compliance sign-off — not just a ~$10.8k top-up.
  • No existing trained roster to absorb launch labour or provide surge cover.
  • Loses the Bankstown catchment entirely — the single biggest gap: zero proof of foot traffic, zero demographic validation at any new site.
  • No food safety / halal cert for a new premises — an added compliance step and timeline the JV has already cleared.
  • Materially slower — likely months, not six weeks, to reach grand launch.
  • 100% of the downside risk alone, with no sister-site cushion and no existing pizza trade to absorb a slow ramp.
  • No "store-in-a-box" replication pipeline — every future site is a full new-site exercise.

Joint Venture — Pros

  • Site, lease, power, counter, and machines already done and paid — remaining spend to launch is ≈$10,800 against $42,190 of retail equipment value already in place.
  • Catchment validated with real data: hospital opposite, ~11,000-student precinct ~600m away, ABS-verified demographics matching the halal-certified positioning.
  • Existing staff roster absorbs launch at low incremental cost (~$4,500/mo soft-launch) plus Charlie's 20-year in-house servicing capability.
  • Six-week path to trading vs a from-scratch build.
  • Shared risk with an experienced operator; genuine second income stream for both parties.
  • Immediate combo/cross-sell revenue with the existing pizza trade from day one.
  • An existing 3-store chain ready for low-cost replication.
  • $179,000 Year-1 base-case contribution modelled with real supplier pricing and confirmed SilverChef terms.

Joint Venture — Cons

  • Revenue split must be shared under an agreement that is NOT YET SIGNED — the share split itself is unspecified anywhere in the source.
  • Partnership dispute is rated Low likelihood / High impact in the source's own risk register; its "sign before grand launch" mitigation deadline had not been met as of 12 June.
  • Dependency on Charlie's site — what happens if Dough Boss Bankstown is sold or the lease ends is unresolved; the licence-fee mechanism is "not yet decided."
  • Staff time from Charlie's roster is "not free," and compensation for it is explicitly unresolved.
  • Brand upside and expansion rights are shared, not solely either party's.
  • The value of Charlie's servicing contribution is not yet quantified in writing.

Collaboration Revenue Analysis

What the partnership itself is worth

The source proposal presents SNOWBOSS's Year-1 numbers ($316,050 revenue / $179,000 contribution) as if they were the dessert counter's own standalone output. Almost none of that output is achievable by a standalone concept on its own site with its own customer acquisition. This section isolates the value created by the collaboration mechanic itself, using only figures the source provides.

(a) Combo / cross-sell revenue

The narrowest, most defensible slice — the only category that structurally cannot exist without both brands present at once: +$5 = 2 slushes with pizza, +$8 = 2 cones, Boss Lunch $14.90, Family Sunday +$10, Smoko Box $45, Office Box $75, and the "Sweet Finish" morning-voucher mechanic (a Dough Boss sale converting into a scheduled SNOWBOSS visit). Quantification is not possible from the given figures — the $316,050 Year-1 figure is a blended units × $7.00 build, with no stated SKU-mix or attach-rate breakdown. Directionally significant, numerically unquantified in the source.

(b) The "existing trade" halo

The site is not neutral ground: the counter is built inside an already-trading, already-leased, already-staffed, already-certified shop. None of that cost or time appears in the ~$10,800 remaining spend — because it doesn't need to. Soft-launch labour is explicitly "absorbed by existing roster." The remaining capital spend contains no customer-acquisition or launch-marketing line item at all — its absence is itself evidence the model assumes the halo does that job for free.

(c) Whole-shop combined-profit uplift — the cleanest number the source gives

Converging estimate

Quantified floor: at minimum ~20% of Year-1 SNOWBOSS contribution (~$36,000 of $179,000) is directly attributable to the avoided-lease mechanic alone — the one number that can be cleanly isolated from the source's own figures (36,000 / 179,000 ≈ 20%).

Directional ceiling (not source-quantified): once avoided site-acquisition cost, avoided marketing spend, avoided full-cost staffing/training, and avoided food-safety-certification lead time are added — none priced anywhere in the source — the true figure is plausibly close to the near-entirety of the $179,000/$316,050 Year-1 case. No standalone comparable exists anywhere in the source document, which is itself the strongest evidence the model was never built to stand alone.

Explicitly not invented: no SKU-level attach-rate percentage, no standalone cost model, no dollar figure for pizza-side cross-sell lift, no agreed profit-share percentage, no market-size figures — none of these appear in the source and none are estimated as numbers here.

Net-margin commentary

At the daypart level, gross margin is very high (~91%) because COGS is dominated by low-cost slush/soft-serve inputs (~$0.63 COGS on a ~$7.00 sale). The gap to EBITDA-contribution margin is labour and running costs: in the Base case, $105,600 of labour + running costs against $287,606 gross profit converts a 91%-GP business into roughly a 57% EBITDA-contribution margin on revenue. The same compression appears across all scenarios — Conservative converts to ~39% of revenue, Strong to ~60% — meaning cost leverage improves markedly as volume rises, since incremental labour is materially less than proportional to unit volume. The operating dashboard's own breakeven bands (34/day at $5,500/mo cost, 52/day at $8,500/mo, 73/day at $12,000/mo) confirm all three scenarios sit comfortably above minimum-viable.

BAS & GST Analysis

This section applies the ATO's general BAS/GST mechanics (verified 1 Jul 2026) to the Year-1 base-case financial model. It is a planning-level estimate only — see closing recommendation below.

1. GST registration — mandatory at this scale

The Base-case Year-1 revenue for SNOWBOSS alone is $316,050 — more than four times the ATO's mandatory GST-registration threshold of $75,000 in GST turnover. Registration is mandatory, not optional, once a business has or expects turnover of $75,000+, and must occur within 21 days of becoming aware the threshold will be exceeded. Even the Conservative scenario ($176k) clears the threshold, so this applies under every scenario modelled. The source does not state which entity will hold GST registration — the JV company structure is "recommended" and "not yet signed" — this is a decision for the accountant/tax agent alongside entity structure.

2. BAS lodgement cadence

The entity will very likely default to quarterly BAS reporting under the Simpler BAS regime (available under $2m aggregated turnover — SNOWBOSS is well under this in every scenario). BAS is due 21 days after each reporting period under the ATO's general rule.

4. PAYG withholding

The Year-1 labour lines ($4,500–$12,000/mo, "casual labour at $35–45/hr loaded" per the source) sit on a planning basis written before 1 July 2026 — the verified wage rise (Section 2) is now live. For any of this labour engaged as employees, the entity must withhold PAYG and remit via the BAS cycle, and meet annual Single Touch Payroll finalisation obligations. This is separate from GST but typically administered on the same cadence — relevant given the source has not yet resolved how Dough Boss is compensated for staff time absorbed into SNOWBOSS service.

Recommendation: engage a registered BAS or tax agent before the 20 July 2026 grand launch. Three compounding reasons: (1) GST registration is mandatory, not discretionary, at this revenue scale, and should be actioned proactively; (2) the JV structure adds complexity a template BAS process won't handle — a not-yet-decided licence fee, an unresolved staff-time compensation mechanism, and a "separate POS department" all carry GST/income-tax structuring implications that need a tax agent's input before the shareholders' agreement is finalised; (3) every GST figure in this section is a range built on an unstated inclusive/exclusive assumption and a not-yet-signed SilverChef quote. None of the figures in this section should be relied upon as final.

Strengths

  • JV Nearly the entire cost base is already sunk and paid: lease guaranteed, electrical complete, counter in place, machines installed, halal certified — only ≈$10,800 remains against $42,190 of retail equipment value.
  • JV Site sits on independently corroborated demand drivers: $2B hospital enabling works confirmed underway (verified 1 Jul 2026), ~11,000 combined students ~600m away.
